1. Frequentist vs. Bayesian Statistics

  • Frequentist : Data varies / Parameters fixed
  • Bayesian : Data fixed / Parameters varies

2. What is a sampling distribution?

A statistician considers this to be one of many possible samples
that you can get. So a different sample of size n would result in a different value
of the statistic s. And actually there are lots– maybe even infinitely– many
possible different samples you could get, along with infinitely many different
possible values for the statistic s.

This collection of all the possible values of that statistic, that’s called the sampling distribution.

3. Why Sampling Distributions?

  • Account for uncertainty by using a sampling distribution.
  • If we don’t have a model for the sampling distribution, we can simulate the sampling distribution through a process called bootstrapping.

4. Sampling Distribution of Sample Means

(1) the mean of all the sample means is the same as the original population mean.

(2) the standard deviation of the sample means– this is also called the standard error of the sample mean– is given by this formula
here. Sigma over square root n. This shows that the variability of sample means decreases as n increases.

(3) the central limit theorem guarantees that sample means will always be approximately normally distributed for large enough
samples.

As we increase the sample size by a factor of 4– let’s go from 10 to 40– we should see the sample mean standard deviation getting
cut in half.

(4) Skewed to the right

  • it’s centered in the right place. The mean of both the population and the sample mean distribution is 1.65.

  • the shape here isn’t quite normal

  • We can increase our sample size even further. There’s 500. It’s a very good approximation

  • we can see that there isn’t much difference between the sample mean distribution and a normal distribution.

    (5) Heavy tailed distribution

  • the tails of the distribution are thicker than they would be in a normal distribution.

  • we get a lot of outliers among our data.
